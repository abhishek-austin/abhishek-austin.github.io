The best services teams I've worked with have the same quiet problem: the work
is genuinely excellent, and it's **custom every single time**.

That feels like a strength. Clients love it. Referrals come in. But it quietly
caps the business, because growth means hiring more senior people to do more
bespoke work — and your margins, your quality, and your sanity all scale
linearly with headcount.

## The shift isn't "do less custom work"

It's finding the **repeatable core** hiding inside the custom work and turning
*that* into a product.

Almost every services team is already doing the same five things on most
engagements. They just rebuild them from scratch each time because it never felt
like a "product." The move is to:

- Name the delivery pattern you repeat most often
- Package it — scope, price, and position it as a defined offering
- Architect it so it can be delivered the same way twice
- Sell the outcome, not the hours

> You don't have to stop doing custom work. You have to stop *rebuilding the
> same thing* and calling it custom.

## Why this is worth the discomfort

A productized offering changes the math underneath the business. The same team
delivers more, with less variance, at better margins — and the work becomes
something you can hand to someone else, which is the real test of whether you've
built a business or just a very good job.

That's the transition I spend most of my time on now: helping teams find the
wedge, package it, and build the thing that lets them scale what works.
